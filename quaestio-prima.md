
# § Quaestio Prima — De significatione terminorum

Upon encountering terms such as *Vital*, *Mental*, *Static*, and *Dynamic*, it is only partially legitimate to take them at face value when one does not occupy an authorial or rigorously analytical position. The problem arises when different socionics schools fail to make explicit the causal and conceptual source of these categories—and instead merely replicate vague and repetitive descriptions. In doing so, the terminology itself comes to be treated as the object of study, rather than as a conceptual derivation of something more fundamental.

Recurring examples of this include statements such as the claim that the Static type “views the world in images, episodes, or photographs.” Such descriptions not only lack conceptual precision, but fail to explain what they purport to describe. What effective theoretical content is conveyed by saying that someone perceives reality in a “photographic” manner? What definition is this, and what intellectually rigorous subject would assert, without further qualification, that they perceive the world in such a way?

[Descriptions](https://wikisocion.github.io/content/statics_dynamics.html?highlight=dynamic) such as the frequently cited—

> *Statics view reality as sets of episodes, scenes, pictures. The consciousness of a Static type is oriented towards perceiving these separate, individual states, and not a continuous flow of changes.*

—do not constitute an analysis properly speaking, but merely an imagistic reformulation of the term itself. The concepts of *Vital*, *Mental*, *Static*, and *Dynamic* possess specific meanings within socionics and were not conceived to be repeated in an uncritical or contingent fashion. Their correct use requires a deeper investigation, whose fundamental question is not the imperative assertion that “they are images, scenes, or episodes,” but rather *why* such expressions were employed and *what* they are intended to formally designate. The substitution of this investigation by circular descriptions constitutes a logical and conceptual error, not a legitimate explanation.
